What is Dividend Stripping?

Dividend stripping (also called dividend capture) is a strategy where an investor buys a REIT unit shortly before the ex-dividend date to collect the distribution, then sells the unit afterwards. The goal is to pocket the distribution payment as additional income.

For Singapore REITs, this strategy has nuances around price behaviour, tax treatment, and trading costs that make it different from equities in other markets.

Two Exit Strategies

Quick Strip (sell on ex-div day): Sell immediately after the ex-dividend date. You receive the DPU but absorb the price drop. Profit = DPU × (1 − avg_drop%) − trading costs. Works best for REITs where the market underreacts to the ex-div (drop < 100% of DPU).

Wait for Recovery: Hold until the price recovers to your purchase price, then sell. You keep the full DPU and break even on the price. Profit ≈ DPU − round-trip trading costs. Risk: price may not recover, or recovery takes longer than expected.

Singapore Tax Advantages

  • No dividend tax: Singapore individual tax residents pay no personal income tax on REIT distributions (unlike dividends from companies).
  • No capital gains tax: Singapore has no CGT, so gains from selling units are tax-free for individuals.
  • Foreign REITs on SGX: Some REITs distributing foreign-sourced income (e.g., UK, China, Australia assets) may have withholding taxes deducted at source before distribution. Check each REIT's prospectus.
  • GST: Brokerage fees are subject to 9% GST — factor this into your cost calculation.

Key Risks

  • Price drop exceeds DPU: On the ex-div date, broader market movements can cause the price to drop more than the DPU, resulting in a net loss.
  • Slow recovery: If the price takes weeks or months to recover, capital is tied up and you face opportunity cost.
  • Illiquidity: Smaller REITs have wide bid-ask spreads, increasing effective trading costs significantly.
  • High gearing risk: REITs with gearing above 40% are more sensitive to interest rate changes, which can depress prices independently of the ex-div effect.
  • Currency risk: REITs distributing in USD, GBP, or AUD introduce FX risk on the distribution amount.
  • Tax residency: Non-resident individual investors face a 10% withholding tax on REIT distributions.

Trading Costs (SGX)

  • Brokerage: 0.18%–0.35% per trade (varies by broker)
  • SGX Clearing Fee: 0.0325% of trade value (capped at SGD 600)
  • SGX Access Fee: 0.0075% of trade value
  • GST: 9% on brokerage fee
  • Minimum lot size: 100 units
  • Total round-trip cost estimate: ~0.55%–0.80% of trade value

What Makes a Good Strip Candidate?

  • High distribution per unit relative to price (high distribution yield)
  • Historical price drop less than 100% of DPU on ex-div date
  • Fast price recovery (within 1–2 weeks)
  • High daily trading volume (tight bid-ask spread, easy to enter/exit)
  • Trading at a discount to NAV (provides a margin of safety)
  • Low gearing (less sensitive to rate movements)
